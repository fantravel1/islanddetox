The Fisherman's Cottage

Stone walls, turf fire, and nothing between you and America

✓ Verified Stay Inis Meáin Ireland

About This Stay

The Fisherman's Cottage stands at the western edge of Inis Meáin, the middle and least visited of Ireland's three Aran Islands. It was built in the 1860s by a man who fished for mackerel and raised a family of nine in two rooms. The walls are local limestone, three feet thick, mortared with sand and lime and held together more by stubbornness than engineering. The cottage was abandoned in the 1970s when the last of the family emigrated to Boston, and it sat empty for thirty years, slowly filling with nettles and silence, until a local couple restored it with the kind of care that borders on devotion. They kept the original flagstone floors, the deep-set windows, the hearth that takes up half the western wall. What they added was plumbing, a proper roof, and a mattress that doesn't require you to have the spine of a nineteenth-century fisherman.

The interior is one of the most calming rooms you will ever enter. The ceiling is low and whitewashed, crossed by black bog-oak beams that are older than the house. The main room serves as kitchen, sitting room, and the centre of gravity: a wooden table, two chairs, a dresser with blue-and-white plates, and the fireplace where a turf fire burns from October through May and on any summer evening when the Atlantic wind justifies it, which is most of them. The smell of burning turf is impossible to describe to someone who hasn't encountered it. It is smoky but sweet, ancient and domestic, like someone burning a very old library in the kindest possible way. It gets into your clothes and your hair and you will miss it for months after you leave.

The bedroom is a small, perfect room at the back of the cottage. A double bed with a wrought-iron frame, a wool blanket woven on the island, linen sheets. The window faces east, so you wake to light coming over the limestone pavement, a landscape so stark and lunar that J.M. Synge spent four summers here and wrote plays about it that still make people weep. Outside the bedroom window, the stone walls that criss-cross the island extend in every direction, built over centuries to shelter tiny fields from the wind. Some of these walls are so old that no one remembers why they were built or what they once enclosed. They are simply part of the landscape now, like the rock itself.

Step outside the cottage door and you are standing on the limestone pavement that gives Inis Meáin its particular, austere beauty. The rock is grey and fissured, colonised by tiny wildflowers in spring: gentians the colour of electricity, bloody cranesbill, orchids that appear impossibly delicate against the stone. Walk five minutes west and the island simply stops. The cliffs here are not dramatic in the way of the Cliffs of Moher across the water; they are low and absolute, a clean edge where the rock meets the Atlantic. Stand at the cliff and look west. There is no land between you and Newfoundland, three thousand kilometres of open ocean, and the wind that hits your face has been travelling across that water for days, picking up nothing but salt and the faint memory of icebergs.

Why It's Verified

We stayed at the Fisherman's Cottage for six nights in late September, when the summer visitors had left and the island had returned to its population of roughly one hundred and sixty. There is no Wi-Fi in the cottage, nor in most of the island. The pub has a connection that works intermittently, but you would need to walk twenty minutes to reach it, and by the time you arrive you will have forgotten what you wanted to look up. There is no television. The phone lockbox is a wooden chest by the hearth, lined with felt, and the owners ask that you place your device inside on arrival. The mattress was replaced last year, a high-end pocket-spring that the ferryman charged double to transport because of the weight. The blackout curtains are heavy wool, woven locally, and they turn the bedroom into a cave of perfect darkness.

Inis Meáin is quiet the way the bottom of the ocean is wet: it is not a feature of the place, it is the substance of it. The island has no nightlife, no restaurants open after nine, no traffic. The loudest sounds are the wind, the sea, and the occasional cry of a corncrake in the long grass, a bird so rare on the mainland that hearing one here feels like a small miracle. The cottage sits far enough from the village that even these modest sounds are muted. At night, the silence has a physical quality, a pressure on the ears, a ringing absence that takes two or three days to stop noticing and a lifetime to stop missing. The food is what you bring or what you find: the island shop sells basics, and a lobster fisherman will sometimes leave a catch at your door if you ask the right person, which the owners will arrange.

What to Expect

A day on Inis Meáin has no schedule because the island does not believe in schedules. You wake when the light through the curtains or the call of the oystercatchers outside your window makes sleep impossible, usually around half seven. You light the turf fire if the morning is cold, which it often is even in summer, and boil water on the gas stove for tea. Breakfast is porridge with honey, or brown bread and butter. Then you walk. The island is only three kilometres long and two wide, but it contains more walking than its size suggests: cliff paths, stone-walled lanes, the ruins of a bronze-age fort called Dún Chonchúir that sits on the island's highest point and offers views of all three Aran Islands, the Connemara mountains, and the open Atlantic in a single, slow panorama. You will walk for hours and see almost no one. You will return to the cottage tired in a way that has nothing to do with exhaustion and everything to do with the body remembering what it was designed to do.

Evenings at the cottage are turf fire and lamplight. You cook something simple: soup from the vegetables in the island shop, pasta with garlic and olive oil, or the lobster if one has appeared at your door. You eat at the wooden table while the fire pops and settles. The journaling kit sits on the dresser, a Moleskine notebook and a good pen, and you will find yourself writing things you didn't know you were thinking. The island does this. It strips away the noise, layer by layer, until what remains is uncomfortably, beautifully simple. By nine o'clock the fire is low and you are ready for sleep. You will sleep here the way animals sleep: deeply, completely, without dreams, waking only when the light returns and the whole slow, perfect cycle begins again.

Getting There

Fly into Shannon or Galway airports. From Galway city, drive or take a bus to Rossaveal harbour on the Connemara coast, about an hour west. Island Ferries or Aran Island Ferries run daily services to Inis Meáin, though the schedule depends on weather and the sea's willingness to cooperate. The crossing takes about forty minutes and can be rough. Alternatively, Aer Arann Islands flies eight-seat Cessnas from Connemara Airport to the island's tiny airstrip, a ten-minute flight over water so clear you can see the seabed. On arrival, the island has no taxis. The owners of the cottage will meet you at the pier or the airstrip with a wheelbarrow for your luggage, which is not a joke but the standard mode of transport. The walk to the cottage takes ten minutes along a stone-walled lane, past fields of grazing cattle and the occasional curious donkey.
